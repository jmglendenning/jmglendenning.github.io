Introduction

The rights to civic participation in the United States has generally expanded over the past century, but the actual practice of democratic engagement remains uneven. Some communities sustain strong voter turnout. Others do not. Some have more stable educational systems, and more favorable health conditions, while others do not. These differences accumulate, shaping civic life. In this report, we have developed a framework to discuss communities with robust participation and supportive institutions as civic gardens, and to those with weak engagement and limited opportunity as civic deserts.

This project builds on the civic desert framework introduced by Tufts University’s CIRCLE, which describes places with few opportunities for political or civic involvement and limited institutional support for engagement. We extend that idea by arguing that civic inequality cannot be understood through political participation alone. Instead, civic life operates as an interconnected ecosystem. Education, health, and participation reinforce one another. Weakness in any one element can undermine the whole system.

Our claim is that these three domains form what we call the Civic Triangle. Education shapes political knowledge, civic skills, and lifelong engagement. Health influences an individual’s ability or desire, since poor physical or mental health limits civic activity. Political participation, in turn, directly affects the allocation of resources toward schools, health systems, and other public goods. These relationships form feedback loops, creating conditions for communities to either sustain civic vitality or fall into patterns of persistent disadvantage.

Despite research linking each pair of these factors, it is unclear if there are any studies examining them as a single, interactive system. In Texas, where demographic diversity and geographic disparities are exaggerated, the interaction of these variables offers a valuable case for understanding how civic inequality develops and persists. County-level variation should provide insight into the interaction of these factors.

To analyze these patterns, we introduce the Civic Garden Index (CGI), a composite measure that integrates normalized indicators of voter turnout, education, and health. The index provides a single metric to classify counties as civic gardens to civic deserts. This approach allowed us to capture the structure in a way that any single variable cannot. Relying on data visualization including choropleth maps, regression plots and other tools, we have revealed the patterns in the interaction of these three important factors.

By treating education, health, and civic participation as a linked system, this project aims to clarify how civic gardens grow, why civic deserts persist, and what levers would be most effective in improving participation.

Theoretical Framework: The Civic Triangle

Figure 2
Expected Relationship
Education → Voting  Education increases political involvement, civic knowledge, and voter participation.
Health → Voting  Healthier individuals are more likely to vote and engage. Poor health reduces civic participation.
Education → Health Education predicts life expectancy, preventive care use, and health literacy.
Voting → Health Policy Communities with higher turnout have more influence on health and education policy outcomes.
Health → Education Healthy people perform better academically; adult wellness supports lifelong learning.
Voting → Education Participatory democracies sustain educational investment; educated voters support public health policy.

The table captures the core ideas: each domain influences the others. Education shapes political participation and long-term health. Health affects a person’s ability to learn and to take part in civic life. Participation feeds back into the policy environment, shaping the resources available for both schools and public health. When these relationships reinforce one another in positive ways, communities sustain what we call civic gardens. When they reinforce one another in negative ways, they form civic deserts.

In this framework, no single dimension stands alone. A county with strong schools but weak health outcomes may struggle to translate educational advantages into civic engagement. A place with high turnout but poor educational or health conditions may not sustain civic participation over time. The triangle works as a system, not a set of isolated factors.

This integrated view guides the construction of the Civic Garden Index. By measuring education, health, and turnout together, we can observe whether counties exhibit balance across the triangle or show strain along one or more of its sides. The framework helps interpret the visualizations that follow, especially when we identify counties that perform well in one dimension but lag in others. In that sense, the Civic Triangle provides the conceptual structure for both the index and the empirical analysis that underpins the rest of this report.

Data and Methodology

Data Sources

This project draws on county-level data from three primary sources: the American Community Survey (Schroeder et al. 2025), the CDC PLACES health indicators (Centers for Disease Control and Prevention [CDC] 2022), and administrative election records from the Texas Secretary of State (Texas Secretary of State 2024). Together, these datasets provide measures of education, health, and voter participation for all 254 counties in Texas.

American Community Survey (ACS)

Educational attainment data come from the ACS 5-year estimates, specifically Table B15003, which reports detailed categories for education among adults aged 25 and older. The table includes 25 distinct levels of schooling, ranging from no formal education to doctoral degrees. These categories allow for the construction of a continuous, population-weighted measure of years of schooling for each county. The resulting values serve as the basis for the Education Index.

CDC PLACES Health Indicators

County health conditions are measured using the CDC PLACES dataset, which provides estimates for key indicators of physical, mental, and general health. Four indicators are used in this analysis:

  • lack of health insurance,

  • fair or poor general health,

  • frequent mental distress, and

  • frequent physical distress.

Each indicator represents a different dimension of community health or access. Together, they form the inputs for the Health Index.

Election Data

Voter participation is measured using official county-level results from the Texas Secretary of State. Two figures are used: the number of registered voters and the total ballots cast in the 2020 general election. These values are used to calculate turnout as the share of registered voters who cast a ballot.

Turnout serves as the primary behavioral indicator of civic participation.

Data Harmonization and Cleaning

The data used in this project come from separate sources with different structures, naming conventions, and measurement scales. The QMD files accompanying this report contain the full code used to align and merge these datasets. This section summarizes the steps in that process.

County Name Alignment

County identifiers appear in different formats across the ACS, CDC PLACES, and Texas election data, which required standard practices to ensure data alignment. This produced a uniform county identifier used throughout the analysis. Since we are only working in Texas and counties are uniquely named, county names were used as the key identifiers.

Geospatial Standardization

Texas county geometries were imported from the TIGER/Line. All boundaries were projected into a consistent coordinate system and checked for geometric validity. A simplified layer was generated for visualization, while full-detail geometries were retained for spatial analysis.

Handling Missing Values

Some counties lack complete estimates in the CDC PLACES dataset, largely due to small sample sizes. Rather than discard these counties, missing values were excluded at the component level. For each index, only the available elements were averaged, with the denominator adjusted accordingly.

Normalization of Indicators

To place education and health measures on a common scale, each index was normalized to the unit interval. This allows direct comparison across counties and simplifies the construction of composite measures.

Analytic Approach

The analysis had three stages. First, the county-level data was cleaned and harmonized to ensure that education, health, and turnout measures operate on comparable scales. This was noted above. This step also prepared each dataset for integration with Texas county geometries. Next, the results were visualized through a set of maps and graphics that revealed both expected and surprising patterns in civic vitality. Finally, each theoretical relationship within the Civic Triangle was evaluated via a combination of visual analysis, correlation analysis, partial regression, and spatial diagnostics.

As noted, we began by standardizing the components that form the Civic Vitality Index. A simple mean of the three components. This index served as a compact summary of civic conditions across Texas. With these analyses completed, we examined how each component relates to the others.

Various plots such as scatterplots with fitted lines, partial regression plots, and bivariate correlations tested the interactions between the three points of the triangle. A correlation matrix, ridge plots, and small-multiples graphics further analyzed the patterns.

Additional geographic analysis enhanced our findings. Choropleth maps displayed the spatial distribution of each component and the combined Civic Index. A comparison of maps demonstrated spatial interaction patterns while quadrant plots identified outliers counties that deviated from expectations. Regression diagnostics and the Morans I test evaluated the underlying linear model and assessed whether unexplained residuals display spatial clustering. Together, these procedures provided a structured assessment of how civic conditions varied across Texas.

Results & Visualizations

Choropleth Maps: Education, Health, and Voter Turnout

The first group of choropleth maps presented the geographic distribution of the three core components of the civic model: the education index, the inverted health index, and the county-level voter turnout rate. Each map used the same county boundaries and a consistent blue–grey palette, so that differences across maps reflected differences in the underlying data rather than styling or scale. The education index was derived from a weighted average of ACS educational attainment categories and normalized to the 0–1 range, the health index was built from selected CDC PLACES crude prevalence measures and then inverted so that darker shading indicated better health, and the turnout measure was calculated as total votes cast divided by registered voters for the 2020 general election.

The education map showed how strongly educational attainment clustered spatially across the state. Counties with higher education index values tended to appear in more densely populated and economically developed areas, while large portions of the state displayed comparatively lower average years of schooling. Because the education index was normalized and mapped with a continuous gradient, it was possible to see not only extremes but also subtle transitions between adjacent counties rather than sharp categorical breaks. This visualization reinforced that education was unequally distributed across Texas, but it already suggested that these patterns alone were not sufficient to visually predict where civic engagement would be strongest.

The second map displayed the inverted health index, in which higher values corresponded to better health outcomes (for example, lower prevalence of poor physical, mental, or general health and better access to care). By reversing and then mapping the index in the same color space as education, the figure made it straightforward to compare “better health” counties to “higher education” counties. The health map revealed a somewhat different pattern than the education map: while there was some overlap with more advantaged counties, the health index highlighted additional areas where health outcomes were relatively strong despite only moderate educational attainment, as well as counties where poor health stood out even when education levels were not especially low.

The third map plotted voter turnout rates as percentages of registered voters. This visualization allowed a direct comparison between actual electoral behavior and the two structural indices. When read alongside the first two maps, the turnout map did not visually align with the education map in the way the standard “education → turnout” hypothesis would have predicted. Counties with relatively high education index values did not consistently correspond to the darkest turnout shading, and some counties with modest education levels still showed comparatively strong turnout. In contrast, the overall pattern of turnout appeared more visually consistent with the inverted health map: areas with better health profiles more often coincided with higher turnout, while clusters of poorer health were more frequently associated with lower participation.

Taken together, this trio of choropleths provided the first visual indication that the conventional story—education as the dominant structural driver of turnout—did not hold cleanly at the county level in Texas. Instead, the side-by-side maps suggested that health conditions were at least as plausible, and in many places a more convincing, geographic correlate of civic participation than educational attainment alone.

Tri-Variable Scatterplots

The next set of graphics consisted of three bivariate scatterplots, each corresponding to one side of the Civic Triangle. These plots were designed to show the empirical relationships between counties on each pair of variables. Each figure used consistent point styling, fitted lines, and axes scaled to reflect the underlying measurement of the indices. Because all three indices were normalized to the 0–1 range (with turnout converted from percentages), the comparisons were visually straightforward and allowed for direct interpretation.

Education and Voter Turnout

The first scatterplot compared the Education Index to voter turnout. Based on canonical political participation research, the expectation was a positive linear association: counties with higher educational attainment should have shown higher turnout rates. The plot did not support that assumption. The fitted line was nearly flat, and the cloud of points showed no meaningful linear trend. Higher-education counties did not cluster toward higher turnout, and several counties with moderate or even modest educational levels displayed turnout levels comparable to or greater than the most highly educated counties.

There was also substantial dispersion around the line, suggesting that education explained very little of the variation in turnout across Texas counties. A few counties with relatively high education stood out with unexpectedly low turnout, and others with low or middling education nonetheless recorded turnout levels near the top of the distribution. This pattern indicated that education, at least at the county level and using 2020 data, was not a reliable structural predictor of civic participation. This finding contradicted the conventional expectation and set the stage for the more strongly patterned relationships that followed.

Health and Voter Turnout

The second scatterplot compared the inverted Health Index to voter turnout. Because the health index was reversed so that higher values reflected better health, a positive relationship was expected: counties with better health conditions should have shown higher turnout. The plot supported this relationship strikingly. The fitted line showed a clear upward slope, and the points were far more tightly clustered around the line than in the education–turnout plot. Counties with strong health profiles tended to cluster in the upper-right portion of the figure, while counties with poorer health consistently appeared toward the lower-left.

The reduced dispersion and stronger linear trend suggested that health conditions captured something essential about the local capacity for political participation. Counties that performed well on physical health, mental health, and access-to-care indicators also tended to be more civically engaged. Conversely, counties with the highest prevalence of poor health outcomes recorded some of the lowest turnout rates in the state. This plot, more than any other in the tri-variable set, visually demonstrated the practical importance of health as a determinant of civic behavior.

Education and Health

The third scatterplot examined the relationship between the Education Index and the inverted Health Index. A positive association was expected: education should support better health outcomes through higher health literacy, improved economic conditions, and greater access to preventive care. The observed pattern aligned with that expectation. The fitted line rose steadily, and the cloud of points showed a consistent upward trend. Although the relationship was not as tight as the health–turnout association, it was stronger and more coherent than the education–turnout relationship.

The plot suggested that counties with higher educational attainment generally enjoyed better health outcomes, though with meaningful variation. A few counties with relatively low education levels nevertheless showed solid health scores, and a handful of higher-education counties exhibited weaker health than expected. These exceptions hinted at regional dynamics—such as health care infrastructure, environmental exposures, or demographic composition—that may have influenced county-level health independent of education alone.

Summary of the Scatterplot Group

Across the three scatterplots, a clear and somewhat surprising pattern emerged. Education was strongly associated with health. Health, in turn, was strongly associated with turnout. But education itself showed no meaningful direct association with turnout. The tri-variable scatterplots, taken together, suggested a mediated relationship rather than a direct one: education shaped health, and health shaped turnout. This relational structure differed from the standard political science expectation and provided the first strong indication that civic capacity in Texas might be better understood through the health–turnout pathway than through an education-driven model.

Comparison Choropleth Maps: Education Index vs. Voter Turnout

This pair of choropleth maps provided a direct visual test of the relationship between education and turnout. If the standard assumption held—namely, that educational attainment was a major structural driver of civic participation—then the two maps should have displayed broadly similar geographic patterns. High-education counties should have aligned spatially with high-turnout counties, and lower-education counties should have mirrored lower levels of participation. The side-by-side presentation allowed this comparison to be made immediately, without the need for statistical calculation.

The left map displayed the Education Index by county. Darker shading indicated higher average years of schooling, normalized to the 0–1 scale. The geographic contours were familiar: higher values clustered around major metropolitan areas, some Central Texas counties, and portions of the Hill Country. Conversely, large areas in South, West, and East Texas showed lighter shading, indicating substantially lower educational attainment.

The right map showed the county-level voter turnout rate in the 2020 general election. Although the color scheme matched the education map, the pattern did not. High-turnout counties appeared in a wide and somewhat irregular distribution across the state. Several high-turnout counties were located in areas with only moderate education levels, and many counties with relatively high education failed to show correspondingly strong turnout. The areas of strongest turnout did not align with the darker regions of the education map in any systematic way.

Read together, the maps offered visual confirmation of the null relationship observed in the scatterplot. The education–turnout association was not simply weak—it was spatially disjointed. Counties that performed well on the Education Index did not, as a group, show the civic participation levels that theory predicted. Instead, the turnout map displayed its own structure, one that did not track educational attainment. The mismatch between the two maps reinforced the earlier conclusion that education, at the county level, was an ineffective predictor of turnout in Texas.

The side-by-side comparison therefore served as a powerful graphical counterpart to the statistical findings. It showed, at a glance, that the presumed direct pathway between education and political participation did not materialize in the data. This divergence laid the groundwork for the subsequent focus on health, which displayed much clearer and more consistent associations with turnout across both scatterplots and maps.

Correlation Matrix

The correlation matrix provided a compact numerical summary of the relationships among the three primary components of the Civic Triangle—education, health, and turnout—as well as several intermediate or transformed variables used throughout the analysis. Because each pairwise correlation was calculated using complete county-level observations, the matrix allowed the earlier visual impressions from the scatterplots and maps to be evaluated in a more systematic way. The matrix also included the inverted health index, which ensured that higher values for all components reflected more favorable civic conditions.

The most striking result in the matrix was the lack of association between the Education Index and voter turnout. The correlation coefficient between these two measures was near zero and not meaningfully different from random noise. This numerical result aligned closely with the flat slope in the education–turnout scatterplot and the geographic divergence observed in the side-by-side maps. Together, these outputs made it clear that across Texas counties, higher educational attainment did not correspond to higher electoral participation. The correlation matrix quantified this null result in the simplest possible form.

In contrast, the relationship between health and turnout was considerably stronger. The inverted Health Index displayed a solid and positive correlation with voter turnout, confirming the clear pattern visible in the corresponding scatterplot. Counties with healthier populations tended to show higher turnout, and this association held despite the wide variation in population size, region, and baseline socioeconomic conditions. The matrix offered a precise estimate of the strength of that association and reinforced the idea that health conditions substantially influenced the capacity for civic engagement.

The third key relationship—education and health—also aligned with theoretical expectations. The Education Index showed a meaningful positive correlation with the inverted Health Index, indicating that counties with more years of schooling tended to experience better health outcomes. This result, while not surprising, provided an important structural anchor for interpreting the other two relationships. Education may not have shaped turnout directly, but it did appear to influence health, which in turn was strongly related to political participation.

Taken as a whole, the correlation matrix distilled the empirical structure of the dataset into a set of clear numerical statements. Education correlated with health; health correlated with turnout; but education did not correlate with turnout. These patterns echoed the earlier graphics and suggested that the Civic Triangle operated through a mediated pathway rather than through the direct relationship often assumed in studies of political participation. The matrix therefore served as a bridge between the exploratory visuals and the more formal modeling that followed.

Distribution of Civic Vitality Metrics (Ridge Plot)

The ridge plot depicting the distributions of the four primary civic metrics—Civic Index, Voter Turnout, Education Index, and Health Index (inverted)—provided a comparative view of how these measures were spread across Texas counties. Because all variables were normalized to a 0–1 scale prior to plotting, differences in shape and dispersion could be evaluated directly without the influence of differing units or natural ranges. The distributions thus offered insight into how unevenly civic conditions were distributed across the state and which components exhibited the greatest or least variability.

The Civic Index displayed the narrowest distribution, clustering tightly around the middle of the scale. This indicated that despite differences across health, education, and turnout, most counties ended up with moderately similar overall civic vitality scores. The index’s construction—averaging multiple components—naturally compressed extremes, but the visual tightness of the ridge still emphasized that only a small number of counties were true outliers at either end of the civic spectrum. The Civic Index therefore captured broad patterns but did not reflect the full spread of individual indicators.

The Education Index exhibited the most pronounced skew among the variables. Its distribution showed a long tail toward higher values, with a substantial concentration of counties in the lower-middle portion of the scale. This pattern reflected persistent educational disparities across rural Texas, where smaller and more remote counties had lower average years of schooling. Only a limited set of metropolitan or suburban counties reached the upper end of the education scale. The skewed distribution was consistent with the cautionary findings from the scatterplots: education differed substantially across the state, yet these differences did not meaningfully predict voter turnout.

The Health Index (inverted so higher values indicated better outcomes) showed a more symmetric distribution, though with noticeable clustering above the midpoint. Many counties achieved moderate to moderately high health levels, while truly low-health counties formed a smaller group. This pattern again aligned with the strong correlation between health and turnout—counties with weaker health outcomes were fewer but significantly disadvantaged, pulling down turnout disproportionately relative to their number. The distribution’s shape suggested that health disparities, while not universal, were impactful where they existed.

Voter turnout had the flattest and broadest distribution, spanning almost the full normalized range. Turnout varied widely across Texas, with no strong central concentration, indicating that counties diverged sharply in their levels of political participation. This wide dispersion provided the visual foundation for several earlier findings: turnout was highly sensitive to local conditions, was only weakly related to education, and was strongly related to health. The breadth of the turnout distribution reinforced the idea that civic engagement was the most unevenly distributed of the four metrics.

Taken together, the ridge plots highlighted substantial variation in turnout and education, moderate variation in health, and compressed variation in the combined Civic Index. These patterns helped explain why some relationships appeared strong while others remained weak: the underlying indicators themselves differed in how unevenly they were distributed across counties. The distributional structure thus played an important role in shaping the correlations, scatterplots, and ultimately the modeling results that followed.

Composite Civic Vitality Index Map

The choropleth map of the Composite Civic Vitality Index provided a synthesized view of overall civic conditions across Texas counties by integrating three underlying components: voter turnout, education, and (inverted) health. Because the Civic Index averaged these elements, the resulting spatial pattern reflected a balanced measure of structural civic capacity rather than the dominance of any single variable. The map allowed for an immediate visual assessment of which counties exhibited consistently strong civic conditions and which faced cumulative disadvantages.

The geographic distribution of the composite index revealed a clear regional structure. Counties with the highest civic vitality scores tended to cluster around major metropolitan regions, particularly in Central Texas, North Texas, and certain suburban counties surrounding Houston and Austin. These counties benefited from strong health outcomes and relatively high turnout, even when education levels varied. Their darker shading on the map underscored how strongly health and turnout interacted to elevate overall civic conditions. The identification and labeling of the top five counties further highlighted how metropolitan or suburban proximity played a defining role in fostering civic vitality.

Conversely, counties with the lowest composite scores appeared largely in South Texas, West Texas, and portions of the Panhandle. These areas showed consistently lighter shading, indicating cumulative disadvantage across multiple indicators. Poorer health outcomes were especially concentrated in these regions, and because health exerted a strong influence on turnout, the combined effect pulled these counties downward in the composite metric. The bottom five labeled counties illustrated this pattern: almost all were rural, lower-income, and geographically distant from major health infrastructure or civic institutions.

Unlike the earlier univariate maps, the composite index smoothed out some of the visual noise associated with the individual education and turnout layers. Counties that had strong metrics in one domain but weak metrics in another moved toward the middle of the distribution. The index therefore highlighted counties that demonstrated consistent civic advantage or disadvantage across multiple dimensions, rather than those driven by a single strong value. This smoothing effect was evident in the reduced geographic variability compared to the turnout map, as well as the tighter clustering of values noted in the distributional analysis.

One striking feature of the composite map was the near absence of counties that combined high health, high turnout, and high education simultaneously. Only a small segment of affluent suburban counties achieved this trifecta, and these appeared as the darkest regions on the map. The scarcity of such counties reinforced the broader conclusion that strong civic vitality was concentrated in a limited set of areas with favorable socioeconomic and health infrastructure.

The composite index map also helped contextualize the earlier finding that education at the county level did not meaningfully predict turnout. Large parts of Texas exhibited moderate-to-high education but only midrange civic vitality scores because health and turnout were the dominant drivers. Meanwhile, some counties with lower education still scored relatively well when strong health outcomes and turnout compensated. This spatial evidence reinforced the quantitative conclusion that education contributed less to civic vitality than health and voting behavior did.

Overall, the composite Civic Vitality Index map provided a coherent spatial summary of county-level civic conditions in Texas. It highlighted clear regional disparities, underscored the primacy of health and turnout in shaping civic outcomes, and identified both the strongest and weakest counties with respect to cumulative civic capacity. As a synthesis of multiple indicators, it served as a bridge between earlier descriptive plots and the later analytical modeling, tightening the narrative that civic vitality is unevenly distributed and structurally patterned across Texas.

Health–Turnout Quadrant Analysis

The health–turnout quadrant analysis provided a structured way to examine how counties positioned themselves along two dimensions that, based on earlier findings, shared a strong and statistically significant relationship. By plotting counties using the inverted health index on the horizontal axis (so that higher values represented better health) and voter turnout on the vertical axis, the figure divided the space into four quadrants anchored by the median values of each variable. This approach enabled a direct comparison of county positioning relative to statewide norms and revealed clusters and exceptions that were not immediately obvious from scatterplots or maps alone.

The resulting distribution confirmed the earlier observation that healthier counties tended to vote at higher rates. A large concentration of counties fell in the upper-right quadrant—the segment characterized by above-median health and above-median turnout. These counties were shaded by the Civic Triangle color scheme and appeared as a dense cluster that reinforced the strength of the positive association between health outcomes and civic participation. Many of these counties were suburban or semi-urban, located near metropolitan health care networks, and displayed demographic profiles consistent with stronger health access and engagement.

In contrast, the lower-left quadrant—counties with both poor health and low turnout—also contained a substantial share of observations. These counties were predominantly rural, sparsely populated, and often located in regions where access to health services was limited. Their concentration in this quadrant suggested that structural disadvantages tended to compound: counties facing persistent health burdens also struggled with civic participation. This pattern aligned closely with the spatial distribution identified earlier in the choropleth maps, particularly the concentration of low-health counties across South and West Texas.

The remaining two quadrants, representing mismatched combinations of health and turnout, contained far fewer counties. The upper-left quadrant (poor health, high turnout) included only a small number of cases and generally represented anomalous counties that, despite facing substantial health challenges, maintained strong turnout. These counties often had either unusual demographic compositions or localized mobilization dynamics—such as competitive elections, strong civic organizations, or longstanding participation norms—that elevated turnout independent of structural health conditions.

The lower-right quadrant (good health, low turnout) held similarly few observations. These counties were exceptions to the broader pattern and signaled areas where, despite favorable health outcomes, civic engagement lagged. Several of these counties were rapidly growing suburbs or exurban areas where high population churn, shifting political dynamics, or demographic transitions may have dampened overall participation. The quadrant labels in the figure highlighted this unexpected pattern and helped distinguish these cases from the dominant health–turnout trajectory.

The outlier labeling further clarified the distribution. Counties farthest from the medians—whether unusually high in turnout despite poor health or unusually low in turnout despite good health—were flagged with text labels, drawing attention to places that deviated most sharply from the expected pattern. These labeled outliers provided potential case studies for deeper qualitative inquiry, as they pointed to counties where standard structural predictors failed to explain civic behavior.

Overall, the quadrant analysis reinforced the core empirical conclusion that health played a decisive role in shaping county-level turnout in Texas. The symmetry between the upper-right and lower-left quadrants demonstrated a consistent state-wide structure: civic participation tracked closely with health conditions. The scarcity of cases in the off-diagonal quadrants underscored the strength of this relationship, while the outliers offered insight into the limited conditions under which the pattern might break. As a visualization, the quadrant analysis made the relationship between health and turnout both interpretable and intuitively clear, providing a valuable complement to the scatterplots and regression results presented earlier.

Education–Turnout Quadrant Analysis

The education–turnout quadrant analysis provided a direct graphical test of the long-standing theoretical expectation that educational attainment should predict civic participation. By plotting counties according to their education index on the horizontal axis and voter turnout on the vertical axis, and then dividing the space using the median values of both variables, the visualization revealed how counties situated themselves relative to this assumed relationship. The result challenged the classic education-drives-turnout hypothesis in a clear, empirical, and somewhat unexpected way.

The overall distribution of counties showed no consistent upward trajectory linking higher education to higher turnout. Instead, the quadrants appeared more evenly populated than would be expected if education were a primary structural predictor of civic engagement. A large number of counties fell into the lower-left quadrant—those with below-median education and below-median turnout. These counties were mostly rural, lower-income, and geographically dispersed across the Panhandle, West Texas, and parts of East Texas. Their presence in this quadrant was not surprising, given that structural disadvantages often align across multiple dimensions.

The upper-right quadrant, representing counties with both higher education and higher turnout, also contained a meaningful number of observations. These tended to be suburban counties adjacent to major metropolitan areas, and they corresponded with the state’s highest socio-economic profiles. But the quadrant was not nearly as dominant as the theoretical literature would predict. In fact, educated counties were as likely to deviate from the expected pattern as they were to follow it.

The two off-diagonal quadrants—where education and turnout differed in direction—were central to the analysis. These quadrants contained the “puzzle counties,” which violated the classic education model:

High education + low turnout (upper-left quadrant)

These counties contradicted the most widely accepted civic engagement theory. Despite ranking high on educational attainment, their participation rates lagged well below the state median. It allowed the analysis to distinguish between the statewide pattern—which might otherwise appear overly homogeneous—and the local variability that shaped civic engagement differently across Texas. The faceted plots supported the overarching conclusion that health was the most reliable structural predictor of turnout but also suggested that contextual factors—urban density, demographic stability, and community mobilization—mediated the strength of this relationship in important ways.

Overall, the county-type comparison provided a nuanced extension of the main health–turnout finding. It showed that while the positive association held across all geographies, its expression varied across metropolitan, mid-size, and rural settings. This helped clarify both the robustness of health as a predictor and the limitations of applying a single statewide model without accounting for local context.

Turnout Rate Residuals vs. Education (Partial Regression: Controlling for Health)

The partial regression plot comparing turnout rate residuals to the education index offered a direct test of whether education contributed any explanatory power after accounting for health, the strongest structural predictor identified in the analysis. This diagnostic visualization isolated the unique portion of turnout variation attributable to education by first removing (or residualizing out) the influence of health from both turnout and the education index. The resulting scatterplot displayed these residuals against one another, with a fitted regression line indicating whether any linear relationship remained.

The analysis showed that education contributed essentially no independent predictive value once health was included in the model. The cloud of points was diffuse, unstructured, and centered near the origin, with no upward or downward pattern. The fitted line was nearly flat, reflecting a slope close to zero. This confirmed the earlier conclusion from the correlation matrix, bivariate scatterplots, and county-type analyses: educational attainment, when aggregated at the county level, did not explain variation in turnout.

One of the strengths of the partial regression approach is that it clarifies whether a variable that appears null in a bivariate setting is simply correlated with a stronger predictor. If education’s bivariate relationship with turnout had been masked by its correlation with health, residualizing health should have revealed a positive slope. That did not occur. Instead, the residualized plot showed the same lack of structure observed throughout the analysis. Education’s weak predictive role was not the result of confounding; education itself did not independently contribute to turnout once county-level health was taken into account.

The labeled outliers in the plot added further insight. These counties were those with the largest absolute residuals—places where turnout departed most from expectations based on health. If education played an indirect or latent role, these outliers might have clustered at high or low education values. Instead, they appeared scattered across the range of education index values, reinforcing the conclusion that counties deviating from predicted turnout levels did so for reasons unrelated to educational attainment. These counties likely reflected local political dynamics, demographic turnover, competitive elections, or mobilization patterns not captured by structural indicators.

The distribution of points also highlighted an important conceptual point: education and health were not strongly correlated at the county level. Earlier plots showed that education and health shared no meaningful linear relationship, and the flat residual scatter confirmed this independence. Because the two structural indicators moved largely independently, the failure of education to predict turnout could not be attributed to overlapping effects or multicollinearity.

Taken together, the residuals-versus-education plot provided one of the clearest demonstrations of the null effect. By stripping away the influence of the strongest predictor and examining what remained, the visualization showed that education offered no additional explanatory leverage. In fields where education is often assumed to be a universal driver of civic behavior, this finding was both striking and consistent across every methodological approach in the project.

Regression Diagnostics for the Health → Turnout Model

The regression diagnostics provided a comprehensive assessment of the linear model that used the inverted health index to predict county-level voter turnout. Because health had emerged as the most consistent and statistically meaningful structural predictor in earlier analyses, it was important to evaluate whether the linear model met the standard assumptions of ordinary least squares (OLS) regression and whether any counties exerted undue influence on the fitted relationship.

The four diagnostic panels—Residuals vs. Fitted, Normal Q–Q, Scale–Location, and Residuals vs. Leverage—together offered a clear view of the model’s performance. Overall, the diagnostics indicated that the health–turnout model behaved reasonably well for cross-sectional county-level data, though it exhibited mild deviations from ideal OLS conditions that are common in ecological datasets.

Residuals vs. Fitted.

This plot assessed whether the residuals were centered around zero across the range of predicted turnout values. The points formed a broad horizontal band with no pronounced curvature or systematic pattern. The residuals were slightly more dispersed at the extremes of the fitted range, which is typical when a predictor such as health has stronger effects for some counties than others. Importantly, the plot showed no evidence of a nonlinear relationship—no U-shaped, S-shaped, or other pattern that would suggest misspecification. This supported the use of a linear model for the health–turnout relationship.

Normal Q–Q Plot.

The Q–Q plot allowed assessment of whether the standardized residuals approximated a normal distribution, as OLS assumes for inference. The points generally followed the diagonal reference line, with only modest deviations in the tails. A small number of counties showed heavier-than-normal residuals on both ends of the distribution, but these deviations were limited and not severe. For county-level electoral and health data—which rarely produce perfectly normal residuals—the model’s performance was acceptable. The approximate normality supported the validity of inference drawn from the regression.

Scale–Location Plot.

This plot evaluated homoscedasticity, or the assumption that the variance of the residuals remains constant across fitted values. The line in the plot showed a mild upward slope, indicating slightly greater residual spread at higher fitted turnout levels. This pattern suggested a modest degree of heteroskedasticity. Higher-turnout counties exhibited more variability in how they deviated from model predictions. In practical terms, this indicated that the model’s predictions were less precise for counties at the higher end of the turnout distribution. Such heteroskedasticity is common in ecological and demographic analyses, especially when structural disparities play a role. It did not undermine the central finding that health strongly predicted turnout, but it did suggest that model-based confidence intervals around predictions should be interpreted with care.

Residuals vs. Leverage.

The final plot assessed whether any counties exerted disproportionate influence on the model’s coefficients. Most observations clustered in a low-leverage region, with standardized residuals near zero, reflecting a stable and well-behaved model. A small number of counties appeared as moderate outliers, showing either unusual health–turnout combinations or greater leverage due to distinct structural profiles. However, none of these observations approached the influence thresholds that would indicate problematic leverage or Cook’s distance concerns. The model was not being driven by a handful of unusual counties; instead, the health–turnout relationship reflected a broad, statewide pattern.

Taken together, the diagnostic panels supported the validity of the linear health–turnout model. The residuals exhibited no major violations of linearity, normality, or influence. The only mild concern was minor heteroskedasticity at higher turnout levels, which did not affect the model’s central conclusions. The diagnostics confirmed that health was a strong, consistent predictor of turnout across Texas counties and that the model’s findings were not artifacts of model misspecification, unusual observations, or violations of major OLS assumptions.

Discussion

The analysis revealed a civic landscape in Texas that is uneven, structurally patterned, and often counterintuitive. By examining education, health, and voter turnout as an interconnected system, rather than isolated variables, the project uncovered relationships that challenge standard assumptions about civic participation. The Civic Triangle framework—our organizing lens—proved useful in making sense of these patterns. Education, health, and participation are deeply intertwined, but their interactions do not follow the familiar, linear pathways often assumed in political behavior research. Instead, the county-level evidence in Texas suggests a more complex, mediated system in which health plays a central role in shaping civic life.

Education as a Weak or Absent Predictor of Turnout

One of the clearest and most unexpected findings was the near absence of a direct relationship between educational attainment and voter turnout. The education–turnout scatterplot showed no linear trend, and the correlation coefficient was nearly zero. The quadrant analysis further confirmed that counties did not sort into the “expected” combinations of high education and high turnout. Highly educated counties routinely failed to achieve high participation, while some of the strongest turnout occurred in counties with comparatively low educational attainment.

The partial regression controlling for health reinforced this conclusion. Once health was accounted for, education contributed no additional explanatory power. The residuals formed an unstructured cloud around zero. This suggests that, at least at the county scale, education does not operate as a structural determinant of turnout in Texas. Local political environments, mobilization networks, demographic churn, and regional cultures appear to play far more decisive roles than aggregate schooling levels.

This finding does not contradict the substantial literature demonstrating education’s influence at the individual level. Instead, it indicates that the ecological level of analysis adopted here tells a different story—one in which educational attainment does not aggregate into a structural advantage in civic participation. For Texas counties in 2020, education simply was not the lever that distinguished high- and low-engagement communities.

Health as the Central Structural Predictor of Civic Participation

In contrast, health emerged as the most powerful and consistent predictor of voter turnout. The health–turnout scatterplot showed a strong positive relationship, and the correlation matrix reinforced this pattern numerically. The relationship persisted within every county subgroup—metro, mid-sized, and rural—and remained robust even in the presence of substantial demographic and geographic diversity. The partial regression residual plot demonstrated that health retained substantial explanatory power even after removing the influence of education, further indicating that health effects were not the result of confounding or multicollinearity.

The health–turnout quadrant analysis reinforced this result, with the majority of counties falling into the expected combinations of high health/high turnout and low health/low turnout. Only a handful of counties deviated from the structural pattern, and these exceptions were instructive: some rural counties overperformed despite poor health, likely reflecting strong civic norms, while a small set of suburban counties underperformed despite good health, possibly due to population turnover or weaker mobilization.

These findings collectively point to a structural reality: communities with stronger health conditions have greater civic capacity. Physical, mental, and general health—along with access to care—shape the ability and willingness of residents to vote. Poor health constrains civic life not only through physical limitations, but also through time scarcity, financial stress, and reduced bandwidth for participation. Conversely, healthier communities benefit from broader social infrastructure and more stable environments, conditions that foster reliable turnout.

The Mediated Structure of the Civic Triangle

The third major finding concerned the relationship between education and health. As expected, counties with higher educational attainment tended to have better health outcomes. This relationship was clear in the scatterplot and confirmed by the positive correlation. However, when connected to the other findings, the structure of the Civic Triangle becomes clearer.

Spatial Patterns and Geographic Inequality

Several maps in the analysis showed that civic conditions are not geographically random. The choropleth maps and the residual analysis revealed distinct regional patterns:

  • South Texas and the border counties consistently underperformed relative to expectations based on health, suggesting region-specific barriers to participation.

  • Rural Panhandle and North Texas counties routinely exceeded predicted turnout, indicating strong local mobilization or community norms.

  • Major metropolitan counties displayed high health and education, but not uniformly high turnout.

  • West Texas and Rio Grande Valley counties exhibited persistent structural disadvantage across health and turnout, contributing to low composite civic vitality.

These spatial patterns suggest that civic inequality in Texas is shaped by both structural indicators and local context. Health conditions predict turnout well in most counties, but political culture, demographic change, and local institutions produce deviations in specific regions.

The Role and Limitations of the Composite Civic Index

The composite civic vitality index provided a useful summary of county conditions, yet its distribution was compressed compared to individual indicators. This compression reflected the fact that few counties were strong or weak across all three dimensions. Many had mixed profiles—moderate health, moderate education, and moderate turnout—resulting in mid-range composite scores. The index, however, effectively highlighted counties with cumulative advantages and those with persistent deficits.

The parallel coordinates plot helped unpack this. High-index counties displayed consistently strong values across indicators, while low-index counties exhibited compounded disadvantage, particularly in health and turnout. Education varied more widely, again reinforcing its weaker role. The composite index did not replace the individual metrics but offered a way to understand how they aligned—or failed to align—within specific places.

Local Deviations and the Limits of Structural Models

Finally, the residual map and slopegraphs highlighted where the model’s predictions did not fully capture county-level behavior. These deviations were meaningful. Counties that voted more or less than expected pointed to local political dynamics that a structural model could not explain. Competitive elections, turnout drives, partisan alignment, longstanding civic habits, institutional barriers, and demographic change all likely influenced participation in these counties.

Structural predictors explain a large share of turnout variation across Texas, but they do not substitute for understanding local political environments. The model fits broadly but not perfectly—and those imperfections are substantively informative.

Conclusion

This project examined Texas counties through the lens of the Civic Triangle, evaluating how education, health, and civic participation interact at a structural level. The analysis revealed a civic environment shaped far more by health conditions than by educational attainment, challenging common assumptions about the drivers of engagement. Although education and health were strongly related, education did not translate into higher turnout at the county level. Instead, health exerted the primary influence, serving as the structural foundation for civic participation across most of the state.

The central finding is clear: healthy communities vote more. Counties with better physical and mental health outcomes displayed consistently higher turnout, regardless of county type, geographic region, or demographic context. Education showed no independent predictive power once health was considered. The Civic Triangle thus operates in a mediated fashion—education influences health, and health shapes civic engagement.

The spatial analyses reinforced this interpretation. While the health–turnout relationship held statewide, consistent deviations occurred in specific regions. South Texas and border counties underperformed relative to their health profiles, suggesting localized barriers to participation. Rural counties in North and West Texas often exceeded expectations, reflecting strong mobilization traditions or cultural norms. These patterns demonstrated that structural models capture broad tendencies but cannot fully explain the political behavior of individual communities.

The composite civic index provided an integrated perspective on these dynamics. Counties with cumulative advantages—high health, moderate-to-high education, and strong turnout—clustered cleanly in the upper range. Counties with persistent disadvantage fell in the lower range. Many counties, however, occupied the middle ground, with mixed profiles that reflected the complexity of civic life in a diverse state.

Next Steps

Several avenues for future analysis emerged from this work:

  1. Incorporate Additional Structural Variables.

The model focused intentionally on the Civic Triangle. Adding economic indicators, racial/ethnic composition, age structure, mobility, and partisan competition could reveal how other structural forces interact with health to influence turnout.

  1. Examine Temporal Dynamics.

Extending the analysis to multiple election cycles would show whether the health–turnout relationship persists over time, strengthens, or varies during high-salience versus low-salience elections.

  1. Explore Local Political Contexts.

Case studies of overperforming and underperforming counties could clarify how mobilization efforts, campaign intensity, competitive elections, or institutional barriers shape turnout beyond structural conditions.

  1. Assess Causal Pathways.

The mediated relationship from education to health to turnout warrants deeper causal modeling. Structural equation models or hierarchical models may clarify how these pathways operate across communities.

  1. Integrate Individual-Level Data.

Merging county-level findings with survey or voter-file data would allow testing whether the structural patterns observed here mirror individual-level behavior or diverge in important ways.

  1. Improve Measurement of Civic Capacity.

Additional metrics—volunteering, organizational membership, local governance activity—could enrich the composite civic index and offer a more complete view of the civic ecosystem.

Together, these steps would deepen the understanding of civic inequality and strengthen the Civic Triangle as a framework for diagnosing community conditions. This project demonstrated that health is a foundational predictor of civic participation in Texas, but also that structural conditions alone cannot fully explain civic life. Local histories, political cultures, and institutional environments remain central. Future work will benefit from bringing these dimensions together, producing a more comprehensive view of how communities sustain, or struggle to sustain, civic vitality.
